Geometric & Prototype Exploration

Basic crease learning · Physical prototypes · Kinematic mechanism

Miura Fold

Alternating mountain-valley pleats on continuous rhombus units. Single-DOF system — geometric continuity keeps panels smooth while minimising weight and maximising structural stability.

Triangulated Pleat Fold

Regular zigzag crease pattern with a single-direction linear folding path. High stiffness along the crease axis — well-suited for rapidly deployable wall panels and lightweight roof elements.

Oblique Zigzag Fold

Diagonal crease pattern whose angle variation generates continuous curved or wave-form geometries. High morphological adaptability — applicable to responsive structural skins.

Diamond Fold

Mirror-symmetric folding of upper and lower units. Balanced folding path with strong reversibility — maintains high flatness at full deployment, ideal for repeatedly deployable roof systems.

Square Diagonal Fold

Yoshimura pattern — intersecting diagonal creases on a square grid. Multi-directional folding capacity combined with high structural rigidity, widely used in deployable shell design.

Physical paper models — from basic pleats to complex hexagonal assemblies, exploring the geometry of deployable form.

Triangulated Folding Concept

Rigid-Foldable Mechanism

The basic folding unit belongs to the rigid-foldable mechanism category — deformation is driven by hinge rotation and every panel remains rigid throughout the folding process with no elastic material strain.

The unit comprises two symmetric rigid triangular links on each side and a central drive hinge, exhibiting 1-DOF kinematics: only one control parameter (fold angle β) is required for continuous deployment.

Geometric relationship: γ = 180° − 2β, where γ is the included angle at the drive hinge. At β = 0° the structure is fully folded; at β = 40° it reaches full deployment.

System Integration · Exploded View

ETFE + Modular Interfaces

ETFE insulating membrane + rotatable modules + fixed functional interfaces. The membrane retracts in the folded state; in the deployed state it inflates to form an air cushion, achieving simultaneous thermal insulation and airtight sealing.

Fixed functional interfaces (power, data, ventilation) are decoupled from the folding motion — they connect through flexible conduits that automatically re-route during deployment without manual intervention.

Connections & Combinations

Joint typology · Geometric assembly logic · Spatial combination matrix

Four fundamental connectivity logics define the spatial grammar of the Foldscape Habitat. Each joint type embodies a distinct organisational principle — governing hierarchy, directionality, adaptability, and density — and can be deployed independently or combined to generate complex spatial programmes.

[1] Radial Hub

Radial Hub Joint

Centred around a hexagonal columnar node that radiates outward in multiple directions, this joint organises functional modules under a single core structure to create a cohesive spatial arrangement. It enables multi-directional expansion within a limited footprint, establishes a clear spatial hierarchy, and allows differentiated prioritisation — effectively segregating functional areas while ensuring relative independence and accessibility for each unit.

[2] Angled Buffer

Angled Buffer Joint

By introducing an angular relationship between two units, this method creates a transitional zone with buffering properties, primarily responding to topographical variations, climatic conditions, and ecological boundaries. Compared to linear connections, angled joints offer superior adaptability and elasticity — maintaining structural continuity while regulating movement rhythms and spatial hierarchy, serving as a vital strategy for linking disparate functional zones or environmental interfaces.

[3] Linear Extension

Linear Extension Joint

Unfolding continuously along a single axis through the serialised assembly of repetitive units, this method establishes a distinct spatial backbone and a continuous flow of movement. It emphasises directionality and order, making it ideal for spatial organisations requiring clear functional workflows or hierarchical zoning — such as laboratory circulation or logistics corridors. Linear extension joints are highly scalable, allowing modular expansion based on demand while maintaining structural logic and spatial order.

[4] Vertical Stack

Vertical Stack Joint

Achieving spatial density and functional layering through vertical superposition, this method couples units vertically to create a tiered relationship between different functions at different heights. Particularly suited for scenarios with limited land availability or high-volume requirements — for example, placing utility spaces on lower levels with residential or observation spaces above — vertical stacking optimises land-use efficiency, establishes a clear load-bearing path, and serves as a key strategy for high-density deployment.

05

Spatial Strategy

Scalable programme across three occupancy tiers — translating functional requirements into a networked system of deployable modules

In extreme and fragile ecosystems, a research station must function as a highly integrated "micro-infrastructure" system. Rather than a traditional room list, five interconnected functional systems define the complete spatial programme — each translated into a network of foldable, deployable modules through the three-layer logic of System–Module–Aggregation.

Research Space
Sample Pre-processing Room Cleanroom Data Analysis Room Data Center Observation Tower

The heart of any scientific station — a controlled environment for discovery. The layout follows a "research workflow chain" from collecting and processing to analyzing and storing, with strict clean/dirty zone separation. Environmental stability is critical: precise temperature, humidity, and vibration control; independent ventilation; and backup power for instrument rooms.

Living System Space
Sleeping Unit Dining Space Bathroom Medical / First Aid Space

The backbone for long-term residents — a "micro-living ecosystem" balancing privacy with social areas so individuals can recharge while the team stays connected. High standards for warmth, soundproofing, and hygiene are essential. Sleeping quarters must be quiet, dark, and well-insulated; dining areas should maximise natural light.

Energy & Ecosystem Space
Energy Pod Water Treatment System Waste Sorting & Compaction Mechanical Equipment Room

The station's life support — a sustainable "technical ecosystem" for off-grid self-sufficiency handling power generation, water recycling, and waste treatment. These areas are usually separated from living and research zones due to noise and vibration. Power units must be fireproof and accessible; mechanical rooms need heavy-duty ventilation.

Community & Psychological Support
Meditation Space Viewing Space Public Interaction Space

Vital for team stability during long stays — symbolic spaces that help people reconnect with nature, each other, and themselves. These areas prioritise natural light, wide views, and comfortable materials. An open feel and plenty of sunlight prevent the "boxed-in" feeling; quiet zones help reduce stress. Warm textures and slightly larger scales provide a mental "breathing space".

Communication Space
Airlock Entrance Meeting / Remote Communication Space

The station's gateway to the outside world — handling people flow in and out while serving as a hub for academic sharing and remote teamwork. In extreme environments this area acts as both a protective shield and an information lifeline. The entrance requires an airlock buffer zone; remote meeting spaces need rock-solid communication systems, great acoustics, and backup power.

Occupants 10 permanent residents
Total Area ~1,000 m²
Staff Composition 6–7 researchers + 2–3 technical/logistics personnel, with possible seasonal rotation

Field sampling and short-cycle sample processing coexist. The logistical "base-load cost" is proportionally high — power generation, water treatment, maintenance, and storage claim a larger share at this scale. Compact integration of basic living and essential research is the key design driver.

Staff Composition Clearer division of roles among research, technical, and logistics staff

Sample processing and laboratory activities become routine and institutionalised. Demand for academic and office space increases. Unloading, storage, and vehicle maintenance organise into a clearly defined back-of-house service chain. Clearer functional differentiation begins to emerge across the programme.

Occupants 80 permanent residents
Total Area ~8,400 m²
Staff Composition High specialisation across research teams, technical platforms, logistics, and security

Research adopts "general laboratory + specialised instrument platform" configurations — cleanroom conditions, constant temperature/humidity control, and vibration isolation. Greater emphasis on reliability of the sample chain and data chain. High specialisation and systemisation across all functional categories.

06

Site-based Design Validation

Candidate evaluation · Site conditions · Final site selection

Four extreme-environment regions were identified as potential sites for design validation, each offering distinct ecological, logistical, and climatic characteristics relevant to foldable habitat research.

China Qinghai-Tibet Plateau

Roof of the World

Known as the "Roof of the World," the Tibetan Plateau sits at an average altitude of over 4,000 metres, characterised by extreme cold, sharp day-night temperature swings, intense UV rays, and vast permafrost. Its fragile ecosystem makes it an ideal testing ground for lightweight, low-impact, reversible prototypes, with integrated solar power enabling off-grid living for mobile research stations and seasonal housing.

Sweden / Finland / Norway Lapland Region

Arctic Circle Frontier

Located within the Arctic Circle, Lapland is defined by long winters, polar nights, and extreme cold. Foldable modules shipped in containers can be deployed quickly, minimising damage to the fragile tundra. High-performance insulation and adjustable shapes allow adaptation across seasons — perfect for Northern Lights viewing, research camps, and low-impact tourism within developed infrastructure.

China Western Sichuan Plateau

Eastern Tibetan Edge

Located on the eastern edge of the Tibetan Plateau, this region features high-altitude meadows, deep canyons, and active seismic zones. As the source of the Yangtze and Yellow Rivers, its ecologically fragile terrain demands structures that reduce seismic impact and adapt to steep, irregular slopes without disturbing soil — applicable to post-disaster housing, eco-tourism, or research outposts.

Antarctica Antarctic Peninsula

Most Rigorous Test Ground

A global hotspot for climate change and international research, the Antarctic Peninsula demands a strict "leave no trace" approach under international treaties. Lightweight modules transported by ship or air drastically reduce on-site construction time and allow total removal after a mission, serving as the ultimate test of structural stability, thermal performance, and energy self-sufficiency under the harshest conditions on Earth.

Site selection in extreme environments is not driven by any single factor — it is the result of multi-variable coupling across environment, geology, climate, logistics, and policy. This issue is fundamentally concerned with structural safety, long-term operational stability, and institutional feasibility. A systematic analysis across four critical dimensions is required.

01 Natural Environment
  • Extreme cold, strong winds, heavy snow, and permafrost exert enormous pressure on structures and materials
  • High-quality insulation and freeze-resistant designs are required to prevent material brittleness and heat loss
  • Wind vibrations and ice loads in high-altitude or polar regions continuously threaten structural integrity

The combination of isolation and extreme weather significantly increases maintenance and supply costs, making every risk more dangerous. Site selection must evaluate environmental loads and material needs from the outset — not as afterthoughts in the design process.

02 Geological Stability
  • Soil stability, earthquakes, landslides, slopes, and groundwater all affect how the ground supports building loads
  • Permafrost melting can reduce ground strength by 20–45%, altering how structures vibrate and react to movement
  • Future permafrost degradation under climate change must be predicted — not just present-day conditions assessed

Geological safety is not a one-time check — it is a dynamic process that changes over time. Even local plants and microorganisms can alter how soil holds heat, indirectly affecting long-term stability of foundations.

03 Logistics Accessibility
  • Very short construction seasons, limited transport routes, and difficult supply chains constrain site viability
  • Helicopter weight limits and multi-team coordination must be integrated into site-selection planning from the start
  • Modular prefabrication — building parts in a factory and shipping in containers — drastically reduces on-site time

Modular hubs targeting zero emissions and self-sufficiency reduce dependence on constant external supply, making the whole system far more resilient. Logistics is not an afterthought but a primary design constraint in extreme environments.

04 Policy & Governance
  • The Antarctic Treaty and Arctic co-management agreements between countries set binding environmental boundaries
  • Laws on transporting, assembling, and permitting modular buildings affect project timelines and operational scope
  • Governance rules often decide maximum project scale and duration of permitted occupation

Because these rules often decide how big a project can be or how long it can stay active, they must be studied during the site-selection phase. Ignoring governance constraints early can lead to costly delays or complete project shutdown.

Selected Site

Origin of the Yellow River

Qinghai-Tibet Plateau Avg. 4,000 m+ Alpine Wetland Eling & Zaling Lakes Near Duoyong Village Eco-sensitive Zone

Located within the core environment of the Yellow River source area, this region carries important natural and cultural value while presenting a practical need for scientific research and ecological monitoring in alpine environments. The site achieves a balance among ecological representativeness, research demand, landscape character, and basic accessibility.

Site analysis diagram. Eling Lake · Zaling Lake · Duoyong Village · SITE
Site Selection Rationale

During site selection, this study focused on ecological sensitivity, scientific representativeness, environmental complexity, and basic accessibility. The Yellow River source region responds well to all criteria. The area contains diverse landscape types — plateau lakes, wetlands, and alpine meadows — providing a strong spatial basis for long-term environmental monitoring and ecological research.

Compared with extremely inaccessible areas, this site retains strong environmental representativeness while still allowing a degree of transport connection and logistical support, making it more suitable as a validation setting for the prototype design.

Site Characteristics

The most distinctive feature is its combination of natural sensitivity and practical supporting conditions. The surrounding lakes and wetland systems introduce complex environmental constraints — changing ground-bearing conditions, hydrological influence, and seasonal freeze–thaw cycles — requiring that architectural design respond directly to the relationships among topography, climate, and the ecological system.

The nearby road provides basic module transportation and maintenance access, while the presence of a nearby village offers limited but essential support in material supply and short-term staffing. These conditions reduce implementation cost while preserving the remoteness and research authenticity of the location.

Relationship Between Design and Site

Design validation on this site focuses on three aspects. First, how the building can adapt to an alpine wetland edge condition through a lightweight and deployable form. Second, how the modular system achieves relatively efficient deployment by making use of existing road and supply conditions. Third, how spatial organisation can create a balance among scientific research, human habitation, and ecological protection.

The site is not simply a background for the design — it is an active factor that helps shape the architectural system itself, and becomes a central basis for evaluating the feasibility of the proposed design strategy.

Design Proposal

Assembly sequence · Outdoor space · Spatial layout · Perspectives

The construction logic of this project is based on a light-intervention deployment system combining off-site prefabrication, compact folded transportation, and rapid on-site assembly — reducing disturbance to fragile environments while enabling fully reversible construction.

01 Prefabrication & Preparation
  • Steps 1–5: Prefabricate structural base, assemble frame and door panels, place boards and outer envelope shell, inspect, lift and load
  • Energy, water, drainage, and equipment interface systems are pre-integrated into modules in the factory
  • Each module receives a coding system and installation sequence to guide later deployment
  • Folding tests, joint verification, and interface testing ensure reliability throughout transport

By shifting complex assembly into a controlled factory environment, on-site implementation is transformed from construction into deployment — significantly enhancing precision and reducing field errors.

02 Transportation & Site Setup
  • Steps 6–8: Transport in folded configuration, confirm site conditions, install reversible point supports
  • Compact folded form reduces unit volume and reliance on heavy transport equipment or high-standard roads
  • Routes follow existing paths or prior construction traces to avoid creating new disturbed surfaces
  • Point supports (screw piles, micro-piles, or pedestals) minimise ground disturbance and allow future disassembly

Transportation itself constitutes the first layer of site impact. The strategy is concerned not only with how modules arrive, but with how they arrive through the lowest possible level of environmental intervention.

03 Module Deployment
  • Steps 9–10: Unload and unfold modules in sequence; fix lower structure to point supports
  • Hinged joints, guiding elements, and auxiliary supports mediate the transformation from compact to spatial condition
  • Inter-module connections support linear, angled, or hub-based configurations responding to terrain and programme
  • Architecture transforms from individual units into a collective settlement with continuous circulation and functional zoning

This unfolding logic resembles an act of "spatial release" rather than conventional building — its advantages lie in operational clarity, speed, and the reduction of on-site waste and temporary facilities.

The outdoor buffer system completes the settlement's environmental strategy — triangular BIPV units create semi-enclosed grey spaces between interior and landscape, while the folding tent entrance provides a secondary climatic transition zone before the airlock.

Design Discussion

The folding capacity establishes a direct relationship between geometric transformation and spatial performance, allowing units to remain compact during transport and expand upon deployment. The modular system balances standardisation — which improves fabrication and assembly efficiency — with site specificity, as fragile landscapes demand careful calibration to local topography, wind, snow load, hydrology, and ecological conditions.

The triangular BIPV buffer spaces create a transitional layer between protected interior spaces and the exposed landscape, reducing direct environmental impact while generating energy and extending spatial activity scenarios.

Future Development

Future development could focus on detailed structural analysis, thermal simulation, BIPV performance evaluation, and physical prototyping of critical connection points. The folding mechanisms, envelope assemblies, and inter-module joints all require further testing at both the component and system level.

The system could also be tested against a range of site scenarios to understand performance under different climatic, ecological, and logistical conditions — developing from a design proposal into a technically resolved and deployable habitat system for remote and environmentally sensitive contexts.

Discussion

Research synthesis · Tensions · Future directions

This project investigates how a deployable architectural system can support scientific fieldwork in ecologically fragile environments while minimising long-term ground disturbance — functioning simultaneously as a spatial design, a construction strategy, an environmental response mechanism, and a framework for temporary or semi-permanent habitation.

01

Foldability as Design Strategy

The folding capacity allows structural units to remain compact during transport and to expand upon deployment, establishing a direct relationship between geometric transformation and spatial performance. At the same time, foldability introduces considerable complexity in thermal insulation, waterproofing, airtightness, and structural stability — it should be treated as a design strategy requiring further development through structural testing, material research, and full-scale prototyping, not as a self-contained solution.

02

Modular vs. Site-Specific

Standardised modules improve efficiency in fabrication, transportation, and assembly. Yet fragile landscapes — alpine wetlands, permafrost zones, plateau environments — demand careful calibration to local topography, wind exposure, snow load, hydrology, and ecological conditions. The project therefore proposes a flexible system adjustable to each site's particular circumstances, rather than a generic structure placeable anywhere.

03

Exterior Grey Spaces

Triangular BIPV units function not only as energy-generating components, but as spatial and climatic elements — creating canopies, covered walkways, shaded areas, and semi-outdoor buffer zones that form a transitional layer between protected interior spaces and the exposed landscape. Entry tents further reinforce this transition, providing a secondary climatic buffer before the airlock, supporting snow removal, equipment preparation, and informal exchange.

04

Future Development

Future work could focus on detailed structural analysis, thermal simulation, BIPV performance evaluation, and physical prototyping of critical connection points. The folding mechanisms, envelope assemblies, and inter-module joints all require testing at both component and system level. The system could also be evaluated across a range of site scenarios to understand how it performs under different climatic, ecological, and logistical conditions.
